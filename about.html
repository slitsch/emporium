Resume (pdf): Sandra "Sam" Litschert Ph.D.


Bio


During her first career as a sailor, Sam encountered geomorphology in action: eroded sediment from disturbed hillslopes and roads washed out into the cerulean waters of the Eastern Caribbean islands to suffocate nearby coral reefs and mangrove stands. With memories of those experiences, she completed an MS in GIS and remote sensing (Forest Sciences, Colorado State University, 2002) and met Dr. Lee MacDonald whose students were modeling the same eroding hillslopes on St. John. Sam worked with Dr. MacDonald as her advisor and received a Ph.D. in watershed science from CSU in 2009. Her research focus shifted westward to spatially explicit prediction of cumulative watershed effects in forested watersheds with field work in the Sierra Nevada of California.

After graduating, Sam’s work has continued to combine spatiotemporal modeling with environmental disturbances, both riverine and hillslope, such as wildfire, forest management, development, and climate change for the Western US.

Outside of ESI, Sam enjoys time with her husband and two dogs: running, cycling, and reading. Also she dabbles in anything to do with fiber arts – dyeing, felting, spinning, knitting, and weaving.
